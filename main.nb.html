Introduction

The average interval from infection by the human immune-deficiency virus (HIV) to AIDS is estimated to be approximately 11 years. major difficulty in studying progression of HIV infection is that an unreasonably long follow-up is necessary to observe a large cohort over the entire incubation period from infection to disease. An alternative to monitoring the presence or absence of AIDS is to follow a surrogate marker of disease progression for shorter intervals.

HIV destroys T-lymphocytes called CD4 cells, which play a vital role in immune function. Disease progression can be assessed by measuring the number or percent of CD4 cells, which on average decrease throughout the disease incubation period.

The project uses the CD4 dataset, the response variable is CD4+ cell count. The dataset consists of longitudinally collected observations on 369 subjects, resulting in a total of 2376 observations of CD4 cell counts denoted CD4 in the dataset. Other variables collected are:

  1. Time: as the time (in years) since seroconversion, where a negative time denotes actual time before seroconversion.
  2. Age: age at seroconversion (a baseline measurement), centred at 30 years of age, so that negative ages denote years younger than 30.
  3. Packs: the number of packets of cigarettes smoked per day at time of measurement.
  4. Drugs: a binary variable taking the values 1 or 0 to denote if the respondent takes recreational drugs or not respectively, measured at each time point.
  5. Sex: number of sexual partners reported at each time point. Looks to have been centred somehow and truncated at ±5.
  6. Cesd: an index of depression measured at each time point, with time trends removed. Higher scores indicate greater depressive symptoms.

Model Formulation

To start with the model selection and analysis, we use the full model with seven knots as given in Zeger and Diggle (1994). According to the fit of the full model, the Age and Sex variables are not significant at 5% level of significance. Therefore, in further models, these variables are excluded. The full model is further simplified to decrease the number of knots and one model with no knots. The following preliminary models are considered: 1. Full model with all the variables and seven knots at equal intervals 2. Model with variables: smoking, Cest index, Drug and time, with two knots at T=0,2 3. Model with variables: Smoking, Cest index, Drug and time 4. Model with variables: Smoking, Cest index, Drug and time with one knot at T=0

The full model with seven knots is slightly better performing model among all the models considered. The model with four variables is better performing than the model with one knot at Time=0. We will choose model with four variables: Time, Smoking, Drugs and Cesd for further analysis as there is not much difference in all the models considered.

We can see that all the variables considered in this model are significant at 5% level of significance. This model can also be written as:

\[ sqrtCD4 = 26.29 − 1.621 × Time + 0.989 × Packs + 1.059 × Drugs − 0.034 × Cesd \]

The coefficients for Time and Cesd are negative, which means that CD4 cell count is decreasing over time and with increasing Cesd score. The coefficients for Packs and Drugs is positive, which means that the cell count is increasing with increased smoking and drug use.

Comparing the AIC values for all the models considered so far, we can say that the random effect model with Gaussian correlation structure is a better model. The Gaussian correlation structure model with random effects can also be written as:

\[ sqrtCD4 = β_1 + β_2 \times Time + β_3 × Packs + β_4 × Drugs + β_5 × Cesd + b_1 + b_2 \times Time \]

The variance of parameter b1 is 3.707 and the variance of parameter b2 is 1.144. The correlation between the two random effect variables is 0.103.
